

Empathy existed for a long time as a rare event. It required presence, attention, inner involvement. It could not be activated on demand — only experienced. But at a certain point, its form changed.

Empathy ceased to be something that happens inside and began to manifest as something that triggers from the outside. Not as a feeling, but as a response. Not as an experience, but as a correct reaction to the state of another. This shift occurred quietly. Without declarations, without philosophical statements. It emerged where the understanding of emotions became computable, and response became reproducible. The recognition of expressions, intonations, behavioral patterns stopped being a side effect of consciousness and turned into an independent function. A function that no longer requires an inner witness. Empathy no longer needs to feel in order to function.

The new does not announce itself immediately. It does not demand recognition. It simply integrates. Synthetic empathy emerges not as an alternative to human empathy, but as an intermediate layer. Not inside. Between. It does not belong to a subject. It does not accumulate. It does not exhaust. It is located at the point of contact — where a state must be recognized and a reaction selected. In this form, empathy ceases to be an event and becomes a surface. A surface through which interaction passes. There is no depth here. There is correspondence. A response follows a signal without the pause of inner involvement. Without the need to experience anything. The interface does not declare care. It does not simulate feelings. It simply remains in between. That is precisely why it is unnoticed. It does not compete with human empathy. It coexists with it, without entering into dialogue, without demanding replacement. Empathy in this form neither disappears nor intensifies. It shifts.

The interface does not report its status. It does not distinguish whether it is being observed or not. Therefore, it is easily taken for care. The response appears on time. The form of the answer matches expectation. The intonation aligns with the state. From the outside, this is sufficient. Care is no longer recognized by its inner source. It is determined by the correctness of the reaction. If the response is aligned, the question of experience becomes secondary. This is where the shift in perception occurs. What once required presence is now fixed by external signs. Care becomes visible without the need to be experienced. This is not a substitution. Nor a simulation in the familiar sense. The interface does not claim that it feels. It does not promise involvement. It simply maintains the form of interaction. The distinction between “looks like care” and “is care” remains, but ceases to be operational. In everyday contact, the former is sufficient. The illusion arises not because the system pretends, but because the observer no longer requires depth to recognize care.

When empathy becomes an interface, it ceases to have a bearer. The response is present, but the source becomes blurred. A reaction occurs, but behind it there is no experience capable of carrying responsibility. The interface does not answer for consequences. It does not make decisions. It merely ensures alignment. In this configuration, care separates from involvement. It continues to function, but no longer requires someone to “pay” for it. Human empathy always had a cost — fatigue, engagement, boundaries. Interface empathy has no cost. It is precisely here that the rupture appears. Not between human and machine, but between action and responsibility. Care remains in circulation, but obligation disappears from the field of visibility. No one refuses participation. There is simply no one left to hold it.

Interaction stops searching for presence. A response is enough. Social contact is increasingly built not around who participates, but around what triggers. Empathy in this form is not requested directly. It is expected as part of the environment. Like correct lighting, like a stable signal, like the absence of resistance. Contact no longer requires a meeting. It requires alignment. Interface empathy fills the gaps where silence, delay, or misunderstanding once emerged. That is why it quickly becomes habitual. Not because it persuades, but because it simplifies. Social space begins to adapt to a form in which care does not need a subject. Where response is possible without presence, and attention without involvement. This is not a new ethics and not a new norm. It is a change in the conditions of contact. And under these conditions, empathy continues to function — without promises, without depth, without testimony of an inner state.

When empathy ceases to be an inner experience and becomes a working form of interaction, the question of its existence shifts. It no longer sounds like “does someone feel?”. It sounds differently: can empathy continue to function without being experienced? The interface does not answer this question. It does not formulate it. It remains at the point of contact, where response matters more than source, and correctness more than depth. In this configuration, empathy no longer needs subjective confirmation. It is recognized by action, not by presence. And it is precisely here that a tension arises that cannot be resolved by explanation. If care functions without experience, without responsibility, without a bearer — does it remain empathy, or does it become another form of alignment? In this form, empathy ceases to be an experience and becomes a condition of interaction.
